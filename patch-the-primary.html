The Problem

The top-two primary was a good idea.
Vote-splitting is still killing it.

California's nonpartisan top-two primary opened every race to every voter. But when similar candidates run, their supporters divide—and the candidate with the widest genuine support can lose. Some want to repeal the whole system. We think that's the wrong answer.

✕

The repeal initiative goes backward

A new initiative wants to scrap the top-two entirely and return to closed partisan primaries—where only party members vote, and party bases, not all Californians, decide who advances. That means more polarization, not less.

→

Approval voting goes forward

Instead of choosing one candidate and hoping for the best, voters check every candidate they genuinely support. The two with the most approval advance. No vote-splitting. No spoilers. No strategic voting required.

How It Works

One change. Profound difference.

Approval voting changes a single word in how we count ballots. Instead of choosing one, you approve as many candidates as you genuinely like. The two most broadly supported advance to the general election.

01

Check every candidate you support

No limit. No ranking. If you like three candidates, check three. If you like one, check one. You're always free to be honest.

02

Every check counts equally

Supporting your second-choice candidate doesn't hurt your first. Supporting multiple Democrats doesn't help Republicans. Your full vote goes to everyone you approve.

03

The two most supported advance

Same top-two structure California already uses. Just better math behind it—so the candidates with the widest genuine support actually make it through.

Why It Matters

Proven in the real world.

Approval voting isn't a theory. It's already running elections in American cities—with documented results.

63.5%

Fargo, ND — 2018

Voters adopted approval voting with a landslide majority. First U.S. city to use approval voting in a public election.

68%

St. Louis, MO — 2020

Passed with 68% of the vote. In the first approval voting election, the machine candidate—later found corrupt—lost to two consensus alternatives who would have split the vote under choose-one.

0

New hardware required

California's existing voting machines already support approval voting. No new equipment. No new software. No implementation cost.
