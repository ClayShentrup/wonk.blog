The Framework

Deadweight loss scales with t²

That's the most important fact in public policy, and almost no one in city hall knows it. A 60% marginal tax rate doesn't create twice the waste of a 30% rate — it creates four times the waste per dollar raised. This single equation demolishes the case for progressive marginal tax rates, means-tested benefits, and most of what passes for "progressive" economic policy.

DWL ≈ ½ · ε · t² · Q
→ Tax inelastic things (land, carbon, congestion)
→ Redistribute via lump-sum cash (no phase-outs)
→ Same progressivity, radically less waste
This is not complicated. It's Econ 101 applied to city government.

The Policy Stack

Every policy graded on one metric

Land Value Tax, Not Property Tax

Land supply is perfectly inelastic — taxing it creates zero deadweight loss. Here's how it works within Oregon law: take whatever property tax you're paying right now and convert it entirely to a land-value-only basis. Going forward, it can still climb each year by whatever Measures 5 and 50 allow — but it can never go up because you added improvements. Build an ADU, renovate your kitchen, add a floor — your bill doesn't budge. Over time, rates drift toward a true land value tax. Speculation gets penalized. Building gets rewarded. And it's fully legal under existing state law.

DWL: Zero

Carbon Tax + Universal Dividend

Carbon emissions are a negative externality priced at zero. A carbon tax doesn't distort the market — it un-distorts it. Revenue goes to a per-capita dividend. Not a slush fund. Not cap-and-trade with loopholes. A clean price signal that lets markets decarbonize faster than any mandate.

DWL: Negative (corrective)

Universal Basic Income

In-kind benefits are the gift card fallacy at scale. Free parking worth $100 to the city but $70 to you? That's $30 of waste per person per month. SNAP, Section 8, Medicaid — all the same problem, compounded by phase-outs that create 60–80% implicit marginal tax rates. Replace them with cash. No phase-out. No bureaucracy. No poverty trap.

DWL: Eliminated vs. status quo

Legalize All the Housing

Single-family zoning is a wealth transfer from future residents to current homeowners. It's exclusionary, carbon-intensive, and racist in effect. By-right ADUs everywhere. Missing middle on every arterial. No parking minimums. No design review delays. Tokyo built its way to affordable housing. Portland can too.

DWL: Reduced

Abolish Public Parking

Every free space is a regressive in-kind subsidy to car owners paid for by everyone — including the barista who bikes to work. Japan doesn't have public parking. Market-price every space. Use revenue for universal cash transfers. The employee who valued that $100 spot at $70 gets $90 cash instead — a strict Pareto improvement.

DWL: Eliminated

Congestion Pricing

Road space at peak hours is a scarce resource priced at zero. The result: overconsumption, gridlock, wasted time, wasted fuel. Price it at marginal social cost. Use revenue for transit. This isn't anti-car — it's anti-subsidy. Drivers should pay for the delay they impose on everyone behind them.

DWL: Negative (corrective)

All-In on Bikes, Buses, and Rail

Protected bike lanes. Expanded bus rapid transit. Grade-separated rail where density justifies it. When you price roads and parking correctly, demand for transit and cycling follows naturally. Portland's geography is ideal. We just need to stop subsidizing the wrong mode and start investing in the right ones.

DWL: Reduced

Renewable Energy & Electrification

My wife works in renewable energy policy. This isn't abstract for our family. But decarbonization doesn't require mandates and bans — it requires getting the prices right. A carbon tax makes renewables cost-competitive without picking winners. Streamline permitting. Electrify buildings. Electrify transit. Let the price signal work.

DWL: Negative (corrective)
Democratic Innovation

Better democracy = better policy

I co-founded the Center for Election Science and spent nearly 20 years on electoral reform — since summer 2006. We passed approval voting by ballot initiative in Fargo (64%) and St. Louis (68%). I know how to get reform done. Not through white papers. Through campaigns that win.

Portland needs two democratic upgrades. First: fund the Citizens' Initiative Review. Oregon already has CIR on the books — randomly selected citizen panels that deliberate on ballot measures. It's unfunded. My proposal: reduce required signatures by ~$200K worth, and redirect that $200K to fund a CIR jury. Same barrier to frivolous measures, dramatically better information. Political science research shows CIR verdicts move public opinion 5–10%.

Second: Election by Jury. Randomly selected citizen panels using score voting to evaluate candidates. Georgia already appoints officials through grand juries. Oregon has the CIR template. Propaganda can't microtarget a jury whose composition is unknown until selection. AI-generated disinformation loses its leverage when candidates are evaluated in person by deliberating citizens.
