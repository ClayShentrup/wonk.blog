For voters

Nothing changes for you.

You vote exactly as you do today. Same ballot paper. Same process. Same local MP elected the same way. Just Vote adds 200 new seats to Parliament — awarded to parties based on how many votes they received nationally. That's it.

1

You mark your preferred local candidate on your ballot — exactly as you always have.

2

Your vote automatically counts toward the national total for that candidate's party.

3

150 extra seats are awarded to parties in proportion to those national totals. Your local MP is unaffected.

You don't need to do anything differently. There is no second ballot, no ranking, no new form to fill in. If you want to stop reading here, you now understand Just Vote.

Just Vote uses the Supplementary Member System (SMS) — a variant of the Additional Member System already used in Scotland, Wales, and the London Assembly, and recognised in academic literature as a distinct and well-understood electoral architecture.

Why 850? Because 650 + 200 = 850 — very close to the current size of the House of Lords (~842 members). The Lords comparison matters: it is very hard to argue that a democratically elected Commons of roughly the same size as the existing unelected upper chamber is bloated or unwieldy. The number anchors the proposal in something familiar and difficult to attack.

Why this reform

Proven, conservative, and impossible to caricature.

The current system was designed for a two-party country. Britain is no longer a two-party country. In 2024, Labour won a commanding parliamentary majority on 33.7% of the vote — the lowest share ever recorded for a majority government. The system doesn't just produce unfair outcomes — it produces unstable ones.

Just Vote brings to Westminster a structure British voters already know. Scotland, Wales, and the London Assembly all use the Additional Member System (AMS) — local seats elected by first past the post, with a fixed number of party seats added alongside them based on vote share. Just Vote uses a specific variant of this architecture known as the Supplementary Member System (SMS): unlike full AMS, the national seats are not designed to correct for disproportionality in local results — they simply add alongside them. This means the total seat count is always fixed and known before polling day. Japan has used the same architecture nationally since 1994, with a more proportional split: 38% of seats allocated nationally versus 24% here. We are proposing the conservative end of a well-tested, already-familiar model.

What killed the last reform attempt. The 2011 referendum on the Alternative Vote was defeated not because the arguments were wrong, but because the mechanism was easy to caricature. Opposition advertising simply showed someone trying to explain how the counting worked — round after round, preference after preference — and it explained itself into absurdity. Just Vote has no such vulnerability. The ballot paper looks identical. There is nothing to caricature.

How many national seats?

150 is our recommended starting point. The seat count is a lever, not a fixed principle — 100 is a viable conservative opening position, 200 is available for a bolder coalition. But 150 yields a clean total of 850, threads the needle between meaningful reform and political achievability, and is the number we think can actually pass.

The details

For those who want to know more.

Everything above is the whole story for voters. What follows covers how the national seats are allocated and how parties may choose to fill them. None of this changes what you do at the ballot box.

Fair to independents

When standing for election, any candidate without a party affiliation may designate a party for their national votes to flow to. This means that voters who support an independent candidate still get a voice in the national seat allocation — their votes are not wasted. This is an optional designation made by the candidate, not the voter, and it costs nothing to administer.

How parties fill their national seats — closed list by default

By default, each party submits an ordered list of candidates before the election. Seats are awarded to candidates from the top of that list downward. The order is entirely the party's choice. No external body determines it and no government mandate applies.

Open list option — for parties that want it

Any party that wishes to let its members choose the list order may do so. The Electoral Commission will administer a membership election on the party's behalf, at no cost to the party. The party specifies who is eligible to vote, what ballot format to use, and what criteria apply. The government's only role is to run the election cleanly and certify the result.

This matters because it creates a path for parties to innovate without imposing anything on anyone. A party could run an approval vote, a score vote, a ranked ballot, or any other format it chooses — among its own members, for its own list. Voters at the general election see none of this. The innovation happens inside the party, where members have explicitly chosen to participate.

Why this is important. The question of which ballot format best represents voter preferences is genuinely contested. Rather than imposing a single answer on the whole country, Just Vote creates a space where different parties can try different approaches — and voters can draw their own conclusions about which parties are most serious about internal democracy.

How the seat allocation works

National seats are allocated using the Sainte-Laguë method — the standard used in Norway, Sweden, New Zealand, and across most of northern Europe. It works by dividing each party's vote total by the series 1, 3, 5, 7, 9... and awarding seats to whichever party has the highest quotient at each step. The result is a fair, neutral allocation that neither systematically favours large nor small parties. It produces a fixed number of seats — always exactly 850 — with no overhang and no post-election corrections. The details are in the legislation; what matters is that the total is always known before polling day.

Why not Mixed Member Proportional? Germany and New Zealand use a system where national seats are designed to compensate for disproportionality in local seats. This produces an unpredictable total: Germany's parliament swelled to 736 members after the 2021 election — 86 above its intended size — requiring emergency legislation to fix. New Zealand has patched the same problem multiple times. Just Vote avoids this entirely. National seats add to the total; they do not correct it. The count is always 850.

Optional threshold: Legislation may specify a minimum vote share — typically 5% — below which a party receives no national seats. This is common in comparable systems and guards against extreme fragmentation. Just Vote is agnostic on whether to include one; it is a straightforward addition if desired.

Why This Proposal

Learning from thirty years of failed reform

Just Vote is not the best possible electoral system. It is the best electoral system that can actually pass. This section explains the reasoning — the tradeoffs accepted, the constraints designed around, and why this proposal sits at the intersection of genuine reform and political achievability.

The Pattern of Failure

Britain has made four serious attempts at electoral reform since 1991: the Plant Commission, the Jenkins Commission, the 2011 Alternative Vote referendum, and the ongoing campaign for full proportional representation. Each failed for a specific, diagnosable reason. Just Vote was designed by working backwards from each of those failures.

Plant (1993) recommended the Supplementary Vote — a ranking-based system — for Westminster elections. It died because Labour won a landslide majority in 1997 and had no incentive to change a system that had just delivered them 63% of seats on 43% of the vote. Lesson one: a party that just won big under FPTP will not reform it. Just Vote addresses this by being modest enough that a winning party retains its structural advantage under the new system.

Jenkins (1998) recommended AV+ — a hybrid of ranked local voting and corrective list seats. It died for three compounding reasons: it threatened over a hundred Labour MPs with seat losses; it used a corrective top-up mechanism that created unpredictable seat totals; and it proposed a system that had never been used anywhere in the world. Lesson two: any proposal that directly threatens incumbent seats, uses an untested mechanism, or can be called an experiment will be killed by institutional self-interest. Just Vote adds seats rather than redistributing them, uses an architecture already operating in Scotland, Wales, London, and Japan, and has no corrective mechanism to generate overhang.

The 2011 Alternative Vote referendum was lost not because the arguments for reform were wrong but because the mechanism was visible on the ballot and could be caricatured. The no campaign's most effective advertising simply showed someone attempting to explain how AV counted votes — round after round, preference after preference — and let the mechanism destroy itself. The Electoral Commission's official leaflet described first past the post in three sentences and required three diagrams and four pages of text to explain AV. A 2013 study found 26% of claims in newspaper coverage were not merely misleading but actually false. Lesson three: any reform whose counting process voters must understand is vulnerable to a complexity attack that is almost impossible to defeat. Just Vote's ballot paper is identical to today's. There is nothing to explain and nothing to show in an attack ad.

The full PR campaign has not succeeded because it asks Labour and the Conservatives to vote away their structural advantage simultaneously, requires a referendum whose outcome depends on genuine government support that has never materialised, and proposes systems — full list PR or STV — that either eliminate local MPs entirely or introduce ranking complexity. Lesson four: asking the winners to abolish their own advantage is a losing strategy. Just Vote preserves every local MP and keeps the proportional component small enough that majority government remains achievable.

What the Failures Have in Common

Reading across all four attempts, five structural failure modes emerge. The incumbency trap: any reform that directly threatens sitting MPs' seats will be vetoed by those MPs. The novelty problem: proposals that have never been used anywhere are easy to attack as experiments. The complexity attack: any mechanism that voters must understand to trust is vulnerable to a well-funded opposition campaign. The corrective mechanism trap: top-up seats designed to correct for local disproportionality create unpredictable seat totals — Germany's Bundestag swelled to 736 members after the 2021 election for exactly this reason. The referendum vulnerability: referendums on electoral reform are won only when they follow a long, genuinely independent deliberative process — as in New Zealand in 1992-93 — and are lost when they are the product of coalition negotiations or party political calculation.

The Design Derivation

Each feature of Just Vote is a direct response to a specific historical failure mode.

Response to the incumbency trap: Just Vote adds 200 seats rather than redistributing them. No sitting MP loses their constituency. The national seats are new, not reallocated. This does not eliminate the incumbency problem entirely, but it removes its most visceral form: the individual MP's fear of losing their own seat.

Response to the novelty problem: The Supplementary Member System is used in Scotland, Wales, London, and Japan. It is described and classified in academic literature — David Klemperer's 2019 Constitution Society report characterises it as "two electoral systems — one proportional, one majoritarian — operating in parallel." When opponents ask "has this ever been tried?" the answer is yes, in multiple jurisdictions, for decades.

Response to the complexity attack: The ballot paper is identical to today's. There is no new mechanism to explain, no new sequence to describe, no diagram required. The most effective weapon in the no2av arsenal has no equivalent here.

Response to the corrective mechanism trap: Because national seats in Just Vote add rather than correct, the total seat count is fixed and known before polling day — always exactly 850. There are no overhang seats, no levelling seats, no post-election adjustments.

Response to the referendum vulnerability: No electoral reform legally requires a referendum in the United Kingdom — parliament is sovereign and can change the voting system by ordinary legislation. Whether to hold one is a political choice, not a legal obligation. Governments have chosen referendums on electoral reform for legitimacy reasons, and a future government might reasonably do the same. Just Vote's advantage is not that it evades a referendum by legal right — it is that it is modest and transparent enough that a government could credibly pass it on a manifesto commitment without one, and that if a referendum were held, the proposal has no visible mechanism complexity to attack.

The Tradeoffs — Honestly Stated

We accepted less proportionality than the math would prefer. At 24% national seats (200 of 850), Just Vote does not eliminate FPTP's distortions. The calibration is deliberate: the 200-seat figure was chosen to be ambitious enough to matter while keeping Labour's seat efficiency advantage in local constituencies sufficiently intact that Labour moderates can support it. That is a real cost, paid consciously.

We accepted FPTP for local seats. Approval voting in single-member constituencies would be a material improvement — it significantly mitigates the spoiler effect and reduces (though does not eliminate) the incentive for strategic voting, and has won 64% and 68% in the two jurisdictions that have put it to a direct popular vote. But pairing approval voting locally with a single-party national list creates a deeper problem than mechanics: it is philosophically inconsistent. The local ballot would say 'approve as many candidates as you like' while the national ballot says 'pick exactly one party.' Voters would reasonably ask why they can express multiple preferences in one context but not the other. That inconsistency is itself a political liability. Approval voting belongs in the next reform cycle, not this one.

We accepted a closed list default. An open list would be more democratically expressive. But mandating open lists would face immediate opposition from party leaderships. Just Vote makes open lists available — any party may choose to run a membership election administered by the Electoral Commission — without requiring it. The competitive pressure this creates is more durable than a mandate.

The Sainte-Laguë Choice

The choice of Sainte-Laguë over D'Hondt for the national seat allocation matters. D'Hondt uses divisors 1, 2, 3, 4... while Sainte-Laguë uses 1, 3, 5, 7... The practical effect is that D'Hondt systematically favours larger parties, while Sainte-Laguë is more neutral. Klemperer's 2019 report notes that had Sainte-Laguë been used instead of D'Hondt for the most recent European elections in Great Britain, the Greens and Liberal Democrats would have had their representation doubled and tripled respectively. Sainte-Laguë is used in Norway, Sweden, and New Zealand. Its adoption here is both more neutral and harder to attack as rigged in favour of incumbents. If preventing extreme fragmentation is a concern, the right tool is an explicit threshold — requiring, say, 5% of the national vote to qualify for any list seats. That is a transparent, principled rule that voters and parties can understand. Using D'Hondt's large-party bias as a statistical substitute for a threshold achieves a similar effect by less honest means: it suppresses smaller parties without ever stating so openly. Just Vote is agnostic on whether to include a threshold, but if one is wanted, it should be stated explicitly rather than smuggled in through the choice of allocation formula.

The Ratchet Argument

Just Vote is a starting point, not a destination. Once 200 national seats exist and have operated for one or two election cycles without chaos, the argument for increasing that number becomes considerably easier. Once parties have had the option to open their lists and some have chosen to do so, the argument for requiring it becomes available. Once the principle that national vote share should translate into some parliamentary representation is established in law, the principle that it should translate into more is no longer a radical claim.

Robin Cook, who served in Blair's Cabinet and consistently argued for reform until his death in 2005, put the strategic logic plainly: the role of a reforming government is to prepare for opposition. You reform the system when you are winning, not when you are losing. The current moment — a Labour government with a working majority, a fractured Conservative opposition, and a Reform UK threat that makes the traditional "keep the extremes out" argument for FPTP transparently hollow — is as close to a window as the reform movement has had in thirty years.

Just Vote and the Current Polling Crisis

At current polling (March 2026), Reform UK leads with approximately 27% of the vote. Multiple MRP models project that on these numbers, Reform would win somewhere between 300 and 400 of the 650 local seats — translating 27% of votes into approximately 55-60% of seats, and delivering a working or outright majority to a party that a substantial majority of British voters actively oppose.

The problem is not that Reform might govern. The problem is that under FPTP, a party on 27% of the vote governing with 60% of seats is not democracy. It is the mechanical amplification of a plurality into a supermajority.

Under Just Vote with 200 supplementary seats, the approximate total seat allocation at current polling would be: Reform approximately 44% of seats (374 of 850); Conservatives approximately 18% (156); Labour approximately 16% (136); Liberal Democrats approximately 11% (94); Greens approximately 6% (52); others/SNP approximately 4% (38). Reform would still be the largest party by a considerable margin, and would still very likely form a government. But the majority threshold of 426 seats would require them to negotiate. The combined non-Reform parties would hold approximately 56% of seats — enough to form a blocking coalition or an alternative government if they could coordinate.

The difference between a Reform government with 60% of seats and a Reform government that must negotiate is not a rearrangement of deckchairs. It is the difference between unchecked executive power and functioning parliamentary accountability.

Conclusion

The history of electoral reform in Britain is a history of proposals that were right about the problem and wrong about the solution — not wrong in theory, but wrong about the political environment in which they were operating. Just Vote is an attempt to take the accumulated lessons of those failures seriously: to design a proposal that does not just argue for reform but actually survives the specific attacks that have killed every previous proposal.

The goal is not perfection. The goal is to get through the gate that has stopped every previous attempt — and then to see what becomes possible on the other side.

Questions

Frequently asked questions

Do I have to do anything differently as a voter?
No. You vote exactly as you always have. Your local vote automatically counts toward the national party total for your candidate's party. There is no second ballot and nothing new to fill in. The ballot paper looks identical to the one you have always used.
Does this require a referendum?
No electoral reform legally requires a referendum in the UK — parliament can change the voting system by ordinary legislation. The 2011 AV referendum was held because the coalition agreement specifically mandated one, a political choice rather than a legal obligation. Whether a future government holds a referendum on Just Vote is equally a political choice. We believe Just Vote is modest and transparent enough to pass on a manifesto commitment without one — but we would welcome a referendum if held, because the proposal has nothing to hide and no complex mechanism to caricature.
Is this proportional representation?
Not fully. This is Mixed Member Majoritarian — the same architecture used in Japan. It introduces a proportional element without replacing the existing constituency system. Seat shares will be more representative of vote shares than today, but not perfectly proportional. That is intentional: this proposal is designed to be achievable, not to be theoretically perfect.
What stops parties from gaming the list?
The closed list default gives parties full control over their list order, which means voters can hold them accountable for who they put at the top. A party that rewards unpopular insiders with safe list seats will face political consequences. A party that opens its list to a member election creates a standard that is difficult to reverse. The competitive dynamic does more work than regulation would.
Why 800? Why not more or fewer seats?
850 is 650 existing seats plus 200 new national seats. 200 is our recommended starting point — ambitious enough to meaningfully constrain FPTP distortions, yet yielding a total of 850 which is very close to the current size of the House of Lords (~842 members). A fully elected Commons of roughly the same size as the existing unelected upper chamber is a very hard number to attack as bloated or radical. The seat count is a negotiable lever; the mechanism is the important thing.
