§ 1 — The Problem

Closed-list PR concentrates power.
Most parties waste it.

In a closed-list proportional representation system, the party list is the consequential decision. Whoever ranks first goes to parliament. The public votes for your party — but your internal process decides who actually governs.

Most parties make this decision through some combination of leadership fiat, committee horse-trading, or internal plurality votes. These mechanisms have a documented, quantifiable flaw: they systematically select candidates who are preferred by the largest faction rather than candidates who produce the best outcomes for the broadest coalition.

"Plurality voting finds the candidate most acceptable to a large bloc. Score voting finds the candidate most acceptable to everyone."

Jameson Quinn — Harvard Statistics PhD, VSE Framework Author

The result is predictable: internal party democracy that looks democratic but functions like a factional spoils system. The party that innovates on this mechanism gains a durable advantage — better candidates, broader internal legitimacy, and a story to tell voters about how you actually do politics differently.

§ 2 — The Mechanism

Two innovations. Either alone
is an upgrade. Together they are
transformative.

01 — Score Voting

Rate every candidate.
Average wins.

Each voting member assigns a score of 0–5 to every candidate for each list position. The candidate with the highest average score wins. No ranking, no strategic bullet-voting, no spoiler effects. A minor faction with intense preferences registers those preferences proportionally — and a broadly acceptable candidate beats a narrowly beloved one every time.

02 — Election by Jury

Randomly selected members
deliberate and decide.

A citizen jury of randomly selected party members — demographically representative — hears from each candidate, deliberates in small groups, and scores using the same 0–5 mechanism. Randomness defeats organized faction capture. Deliberation produces informed decisions. The result is a list that reflects the whole party, not just who showed up to the caucus with the most bodies.

These can be deployed separately or together. Score voting alone is a significant upgrade to any internal vote. Election by Jury alone defeats organized bloc control. Combining them produces a system that is mathematically resistant to the failure modes that plague every conventional internal party election.

"Election by Jury is sortition applied to the consequential decision. The randomly selected deliberative body is, by construction, representative of the party as a whole — not of whichever faction mobilized most effectively."

Clay Shentrup — co-founder, Center for Election Science · electionbyjury.org

How it works in practice

Candidates submit profiles and answer standardized questions

The jury receives written materials, video statements, and structured Q&A. Everyone works from the same information set — no advantage for whoever has the most charisma in a room.

A randomly selected jury of 20–30 party members deliberates

Selected by stratified random sampling to reflect the party's demographic composition. They meet for one to three days, in person or hybrid. Small-group deliberation before scoring.

Each juror scores every candidate 0–5

Simultaneously and independently. Average scores determine list order. The result is published with full transparency — every juror's scores, every candidate's average, the deliberation summary.

The party ratifies and the list is set

Leadership reviews for compliance with any statutory or party-rule constraints, then ratifies. The deliberative record is public. Any challenge must engage with the actual scores and deliberation — not just the outcome.

VSE measures what fraction of the theoretically possible voter satisfaction a given method captures. The gap between score voting and plurality is not marginal — it is the difference between a mechanism that works and one that systematically fails.

The academic foundation here is unusually strong. Jameson Quinn's VSE framework is the field's standard comparative benchmark. Warren D. Smith's Bayesian regret calculations underpin Gaming the Vote, the definitive popular treatment of the subject. These are not fringe researchers — this is the mathematical consensus on voting systems, simply not yet translated into party practice.

§ 4 — Why This Matters for Your Party

The party that does this first
owns the story.

In a PR system, voters already understand that the party list is where power lives. A party that can credibly say "we select our candidates through a deliberative citizen jury using score voting" is making a claim that no other party can match — and one that is essentially impossible to attack, because the mechanism is transparently more democratic than the alternative.

The internal benefit

Factions that lose under the current system cannot claim the result was rigged when the scores are published and the jury was randomly selected. Legitimacy is built into the mechanism. Internal cohesion improves because the losers participated in a process they cannot dispute.

The external benefit

Voters in every PR country are skeptical of party list manipulation. A party that has demonstrably solved this problem — with a verifiable, transparent mechanism — has a genuine differentiator. Not a slogan. A process.
