so you liked the design of wonk.blog and you want to know how it was made. flattering. genuinely. here you go.

every color, every font, every decision is documented below with a completely real but also kind of unhinged backstory. because good design has a reason, even when the reason is "it just felt right and i needed to justify it after the fact."

note: no a.i. was consulted on the aesthetic opinions expressed herein.

the colors

#080808

almost-black

background / void

not #000000. never #000000. pure black is for people who haven't thought about it. this is the color of a cinema at 2 a.m. after everyone's left. it's warm in a way you can't quite explain. designers call this "softening the void." i call it not being a psychopath.

#f0ead8

vellum

body text / primary foreground

this is the color of old paper. specifically, the paper in a 16th-century venetian printing house. the kind gutenberg's descendants were using to spread ideas that got people in trouble. we are continuing that tradition. also, white text on black backgrounds gives people headaches. this doesn't.

#c6ff2f

acid green

accent / emphasis / "look here"

chartreuse is the only color named after a liqueur made by monks in the french alps since 1737. it's also the color used on high-visibility safety vests, rave flyers, and early-2000s web design that was ahead of its time. this shade is that color but angrier. it means business. it's the color of a correct argument.

#d4542a

rust

secondary accent / warmth / italics

terracotta. the color of roman roof tiles. of the clay pots that the mediterranean world shipped olive oil in for a thousand years. if acid green is the sharp argument, rust is the long historical view — the "people have been getting this wrong for millennia" energy. it keeps the page from looking like a hacker's monitor.

#222222

graphite

borders / structural lines

you know how a good suit has contrast stitching that you only notice on close inspection? that's this. it's not a color that demands attention. it's doing pure structural work — dividing space, suggesting hierarchy, making the grid visible without being loud about it. the unsung hero of the whole palette.

the fonts

unbounded

geometric sans-serif / display

designed by masha cairo for google fonts, released 2022. it's named "unbounded" because the letters have no descenders and extend upward without constraint — like an argument that keeps escalating at thanksgiving dinner.

the real reason it's here: it looks like a font that would be on the cover of a soviet-era technical manual about optimal collective farm output allocation, but make it fashion. bauhaus meets brutalism meets "i have opinions about land value taxation."

used for: wordmark · headings · topic cards · stats
weights: 300 (light italic) · 700 (bold) · 900 (black)

fixing
broken
systems.
the light italic is for when you want to be emphatic but also a little wistful about it

lora

contemporary serif / body text

designed by olga karpushina, lora was built specifically for reading on screens — not scanned from a metal type specimen, but drawn from scratch with pixel grids in mind. it has calligraphic roots (think: someone who actually studied penmanship) but it's been optimized for a medium that didn't exist when serifs were invented.

the long arc of typographic history bending toward legibility. also the italic is extremely beautiful and i would die for it.

used for: body copy · manifesto text · blockquotes · this very sentence
weights: 400 (regular) · 400i (italic) · 600 (semibold)

most political disagreement is a category error — conflating empirical questions about mechanism efficiency with normative questions about redistribution. separate those two things and most of the fighting disappears.

"voter satisfaction efficiency doesn't care about your feelings about i.r.v. it's a number."

jetbrains mono

monospace / data / metadata

literally made by the company that makes i.d.e.s for programmers. it was designed after jetbrains analyzed how developers actually read code — and added subtle ligatures and increased x-height specifically to reduce eye strain at 2 a.m. when you're debugging something stupid.

on this site it plays a different role: it's the voice of the data, the labels, the slightly-too-earnest metadata. the font that says "i measured this and here is the number." which, given the whole thesis of the site, feels right.

used for: eyebrows · stat labels · nav links · tags · this parenthetical
weights: 400 · 700

the principles (hover for emphasis)

01

everything lowercase

capitalization is a power move. it implies that some words are more important than others based on their position in a sentence rather than their actual content. we disagree with this ontologically. initialisms get periods because i.r.v. looks more human than IRV and we are, allegedly, humans.

02

grid as argument

the hard-edged grid isn't just aesthetic — it's the visual equivalent of a logical structure. borders don't blur. columns don't drift. everything is where it says it is. this is what policy arguments should look like too, but that's a longer essay.

03

noise and warmth

the grain texture overlay, the off-black background, the cream text — these exist to prevent the page from feeling like a u.i. kit. screens are cold. ideas are warm. the job of the design is to not get in the way of the ideas, and pure cold black-and-white absolutely gets in the way.
