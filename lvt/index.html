solving the 145-year assessment problem through competitive bidding

the problem

since henry george in 1879, economists have agreed: tax land values, not improvements. land value tax (l.v.t.) could fund public goods without deadweight loss.

so why hasn't it been implemented at scale?

the assessment problem

bureaucrats can't separate land value from improvements. it's expensive, imprecise, politically contentious. every assessment leads to disputes and litigation.

competitive bidding solves this. the market discovers land values directly. no assessors needed.

how it works

bidders submit two components

land value: how much they value the bare land
improvement value: how much they value existing structures

highest total bidder wins

land + improvements = total bid

winner pays second-highest total

vickrey auction mechanism makes truthful bidding the dominant strategy

payment splits two ways

government receives the second-highest land bid — market land value
incumbent receives the remainder

if incumbent refuses

improvements are destroyed, bare land auctioned to highest land bidder at second-highest land price

worked example

bidder land value improvement value total result
alice $1.0m $1.3m $2.3m wins
bob $1.5m $0.7m $2.2m second-highest
charlie $0.9m $1.0m $1.9m third

outcome

alice wins, pays $2.2m (second-highest total)

government receives $1.5m — bob's land bid, second-highest land value

incumbent receives $0.7m — remainder after government takes land value

government captures true market land value through competitive bidding

why this works

perfect investment incentives

when you improve property (add $150k value), competitive bidding raises the second-highest total. you capture that value when selling. the land tax acts as a lump sum that doesn't affect marginal returns — same investment incentives as fee simple ownership.

government captures market land value

government receives second-highest land bid — true market valuation revealed through competitive bidding. when a subway gets built nearby and land values rise $1m, government's share rises by $1m. land appreciation flows to public, not private owners.

no bureaucratic assessment

competitive bidding reveals both land and improvement values directly. no assessors, no appeals, no valuation disputes. the market discovers prices through auction mechanism.

honest bidding enforced by game theory

vickrey second-price makes truthful total bidding optimal. the bare land auction fallback creates incentive for honest component bidding — you might win just the land, and your stated land value matters for that contingency.

solves the 145-year assessment problem

since henry george in 1879, l.v.t. couldn't be implemented because bureaucrats can't separate land from improvements. this mechanism solves it through market competition rather than bureaucratic estimation.

frequently asked questions

what if i build a house and a developer tears it down?

developer bids: land $2m, improvements $0, total $2m. you get improvement payment based on remainder after government takes land value. this teardown risk is identical to fee simple — if the market shifts to higher-value use, you lose your improvement investment. the mechanism doesn't eliminate that risk. it replicates fee simple risk while redirecting land appreciation to public revenue.

why would i bid my components honestly?

total bidding: standard vickrey logic — you pay second-price regardless of your stated value, so truthful bidding is optimal.

component bidding: if you don't win total property and incumbent refuses other offers, bare land goes to auction. you might win that auction. your stated land value determines your payment there. vickrey logic applies — honest land bidding is optimal for that contingency.

how often would bidding occur?

continuous bidding (monthly or quarterly rounds). if you're outbid, you get 30 days notice to find another property or recruit a competing bidder. the system uses the 30-year treasury rate to convert between monthly rent (flow) and present value (stock).

what prevents collusion or gaming?

vickrey auctions are strategy-proof — truthful bidding is dominant regardless of what others do. the bare land fallback creates mutually assured destruction dynamics: incumbent can recruit competing bidders if winner lowballs. both parties prefer honest dealing.

how much revenue would this generate?

gaffney (2009) estimates u.s. land value at $23 trillion. at 5% annual return: $1.15 trillion per year. that's roughly 6% of g.d.p. — enough to fund substantial u.b.i., replace distortionary taxes, or invest in infrastructure.

how is this different from harberger taxation?

harberger: self-assess total property value, pay percentage tax (bureaucrat-chosen rate), anyone can force-purchase at your stated price. problems: taxes improvements, requires choosing a tax rate.

this mechanism: bid components separately, pay second-price, only market-determined values (plus treasury rate for the present-value conversion). explicitly separates land from improvements.

the bigger picture

this isn't just about land taxation. it's about mechanism design — using auction theory and game theory to solve problems bureaucratic systems can't.

we took a 145-year-old problem (separating land from improvements for assessment) and solved it through competitive bidding. vickrey auctions make honest bidding optimal. m.a.d. enforcement ensures rational acceptance. the market discovers all values directly.

the tools are simple: vickrey auctions (1961), mutually assured destruction dynamics, and present value calculations (econ 101). nothing exotic. just careful thinking about incentives and efficiency.

pure georgist l.v.t. with no bureaucratic assessment. government captures market land value. investment incentives preserved. best of both worlds.
